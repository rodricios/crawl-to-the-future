UPDATE
Police Arrest 10 Marchers Protesting Gore's Oil Ties

By JOHNATHON BRIGGS and ERIC MALNIC, Times Staff Writers

      Police confronted and arrested a small group of traffic-blocking demonstrators this afternoon during a "March Against Big Oils Buyout of Al Gore" just minutes before the opening session of the Democratic National Convention in Los Angeles.
     The trouble started at 12:45 p.m., when about 10 of approximately a thousand marchers sat down on the pavement at 6th and Flower streets, a main downtown intersection, locking arms and refusing to move. All the protesters had marched from Pershing Square, a downtown Los Angeles park, to the convention site at Staples Center and were headed back to the park when the confrontation began.
     About 30 helmeted police surrounded the seated demonstrators. The officers swiftly cleared out news reporters and other marchers, many of whom screamed and shouted as they fled from the intersection. The move effectively parted the sea of demonstrators, leaving the 10 protesters in the middle, seated around a cardboard oil derrick they had erected in the intersection.
     Dan Denvir, 17, of Washington, D.C., one of the marchers cleared from the intersection, said he was struck in the chest by a police baton.
     "I was standing there peaceful in line and a cop with a real angry look on his face came running at me, " Denvir said. "I got pushed to the ground and clubbed."
     Denvir displayed a large, red welt on his chest.
     A few minutes later, the officers moved in and arrested the seated protesters. They were taken into custody without resistance, chanting "Gore, what about Oxy?" as they left.
     Officers using bullhorns ordered the demonstrators on either side of the intersection to return immediately to Pershing Square or be arrested.
     An observer with the National Trial Lawyers group said she saw a demonstrator whose signpost caught on a light standard. As his sign pitched forward, a police officer grabbed it and a brief struggle over the sign ensued. The demonstrator was pushed to the ground.
     The remaining marchers, who clapped and cheered their arrested colleagues, then dispersed. Many immediately headed for the square, where folk singer Bonnie Raitt was scheduled to give a performance in support of saving forests.
     Reacting to "the unusual occurrences" in the protest area, police at 1:30 p.m. declared a "full tactical alert," under which officers respond only to priority calls such as felonies, emergencies and people in danger or trouble.
     Thousands of office workers in high-rise buildings lining the mile-long march route gawked from windows as the demonstrators passed. Unlike Sundays protest, no merchants shut their shops.
     Scores of riot-gear-clad police lined the route, watching carefully. The demonstrators said they objected to Gores ownership of stock in Occidental Petroleum, which is planning an exploration of potential oil fields under the forests of northeastern Colombia.
     A man walking beside an electric car carrying a solar-powered sound system led the crowd in chants. Example: "Gores Got Stocks in Oxys Murder Plot."
     One marcher, a middle-age man in a shirt and tie, told a group of officers, "You guys are doing a good job, keep up the good work."
     But one self-described anarchist, dressed in a clown suit, disagreed.
     "You should be ashamed of yourselves," he told some cops, as he took a bite of a sandwich.
     At about 1:30 p.m., Joe Andrew, national chairman of the Democratic National Committee, hammered his gavel to open the convention.
     Moments later, Cardinal Roger M. Mahony, the archbishop of Los Angeles, became the first Roman Catholic prelate in at least 16 years to deliver the invocation at a Democratic convention. Although several Catholic bishops have prayed at Republican conventions during that time, the Democratic partys pro-choice stand on abortion is widely believed to have been the reason for the de facto Catholic boycott.
     In his prayer, Mahony made clear the churchs continuing opposition to abortion.
     "We pray that your Spirit will inspire all candidates, regardless of party, to embody in their words, actions, and policies values that protect all human life, establish peace, promote justice, and uphold the common good," Mahony prayed.
     "In You, O God, we trust that you will keep us ever committed to protect the life and well-being of all people, but especially unborn children, the sick and the elderly, those on skid row and those on death row."
     Several hours earlier, before the march began, the atmosphere outside of Staples had been a cross between a street festival and protest rally as demonstrators gathered at Pershing Square, which has been serving as one of the principal rallying points for demonstrators.
     Puppeteers readied their marionettes. Organizers handed press packets to reporters. One black-clad man held an American flag that had dollar signs on the field of stars.
     The messages of the morning were predominantly anti-government and anti-big business. Amid cheers, Medea Benjamin, Green Party candidate for the U.S. Senate in California, told the sometimes boisterous crowd that the Republican and Democrats were "both oil company parties."
     "Nowhere is the corporate stranglehold on our democracy more evident than in Al Gores relationship with Occidental Petroleum," said John Sellers of the Ruckus Society. Seller had been arrested for protesting at the Republican convention in Philadelphia.
     "We must throw our bodies in the path of these modern day conquistadors," said Sellers.
     Sandra Alvarez of Global Exchange, a San Francisco-based nonprofit human rights group, told the gathering crowd that the drug war "is just a guise."
     She said oil barons "want to take control . . . so they can exploit the petroleum and make money from the [drug] war."
     Hours earlier, excited music fans began gathering at the designated protest zone across the street from the convention site, hoping to secure good spots for tonights free concert by Rage Against the Machine, a nationally acclaimed rock band.
     The Los Angeles quartet, known for its radical leftist bent and the sonic blast of its rap-rock, is expected to begin the concert about the same time that President Clinton is scheduled to deliver his valedictory political address to the convention.
     The Los Angeles Police Department is worried about the concert, fearing it will attract a huge and volatile crowd prone to dangerous behavior, said LAPD Cmdr. Dave Kalish.
     "It appears the organizers are taking advantage of the situation," Kalish said Friday. "Were gravely concerned because of security reason--just the large number of individuals that this will bring out."
     The bands Web site encourages civil disobedience to draw attention to societal ills. The group has championed numerous causes, from protests against U.S. military policies to the push for the retrial of convicted cop-killer Mumia Abu-Jamal--the theme of a protest march in downtown Los Angeles on Sunday.
     Juan Nunez and Christopher Martinez, both 18, said they arrived at the protest site about 4 a.m. today, bringing along bottled water and ham sandwiches to sustain them through the 16-hour wait.
     They said they planned to vote for Gore and Lieberman, but they were not that interested in the convention proceedings across the street.
     A Waltham, Mass. group called the Alliance for Democracy set up a 27-foot-tall balloon shaped like the Liberty Bell across from the convention hall. The balloon sported signs saying, "Our country is not for sale. Save our democracy. Full public funding for public elections."
     Demonstrations scheduled later today include a march against the World Trade Organization and corporate globalization; two marches against corporate greed; a protest against the exclusion of Green Party candidate Ralph Nader from the presidential debates; and the beginning of a four-day unity rally.
     Security has been noticeably tightened at key federal buildings across Los Angeles this week because of the convention.
     Mary Filippini, spokeswoman for the federal General Services Administration in San Francisco, said Federal Protective Services officers from Washington and nine of the agencys 10 regions were sent to beef up security at the main federal facilities in Los Angeles.
     "Generally, when we have events of this nature--the Olympics or conventions or anything that could call attention to federal buildings--we do beef up our cadre of officers in the area," Filippini said.
