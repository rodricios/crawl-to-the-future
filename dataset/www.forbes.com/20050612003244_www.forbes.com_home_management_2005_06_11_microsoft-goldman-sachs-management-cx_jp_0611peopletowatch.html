People To Watch
The Week Ahead: June 13-17, 2005
By Forbes.com staff, 06.11.05, 6:00 AM ET

A look ahead to the week's doers and doings in business, entertainment and technology:



Background on Bonnell 		When to watch: Tuesday, June 14. It's game over for James Caparro, former president and chief executive of gamemaker Atari (nasdaq: ATAR - news - people ), who resigned on June 7 after only seven months at the helm. Caparro is moving to the CEO gig at Entertainment Distribution, a division of Glenayre Technologies (nasdaq: GEMS - news - people ), leaving Atari Chairman and Chief Creative Officer Bruno Bonnell to stand in until the board can find a permanent replacement. Fortunately, Bonnell's no stranger to running the show: In 1983, he co-founded IESA, the company that would eventually become Atari, and he served as CEO until Caparro took the job in November 2004. Now, just one week into his new tenure, Bonnell will preside over Atari's fourth-quarter earnings release. Analysts expect the company to post a loss of 4 cents per share on $76.4 million in revenue. That's in the mid-range of the company's guidance for the quarter, and up from a loss of 16 cents in the same quarter last year. Don't expect analysts and investors to take it easy on Bonnell during the earnings call; Atari shares are trading at about half the value they were two years ago. The company makes games for both computers and videogame consoles, including Sony's (nyse: SNE - news - people ) PlayStation, Microsoft's (nasdaq: MSFT - news - people ) Xbox and Nintendo's (otc: NTDOY - news - people ) GameCube, so investors will likely be curious about what titles are planned for the latest generation of those consoles. --David M. Ewalt
When to watch: Tuesday, June 14. Adware and spyware, often bespoken in the same exasperated breath, may overlap--but there's a vast difference between a nuisance and a crime. Intermix Media (amex: MIX - news - people ) is an Internet entity with the mission of providing "marketers, advertisers, agencies and Web publishers with access to the mass consumer market." What all that may boil down to is adware, which introduces annoying but lucrative pop-up ads onto Web surfers' PCs. Investors will find out just how lucrative when the firm, led by Chief Executive Richard M. Rosenblatt, releases earnings on Tuesday. New York State Attorney General Eliot Spitzer, however, is more interested in whether Intermix is crossing to the dark side. On April 28, he filed a suit against the company, alleging that its software behaves like spyware, parasitically insinuating itself inside PCs, slowing performance and sapping productivity. Intermix denies culpability. In a statement, the firm said the matters alleged in the suit occurred under its prior management and insisted it halted distribution of the suspect software in April. Spitzer's attention has not gone unnoticed by Intermix rivals: Daniel Todd of adware firm 180 Solutions hastened to point out his company's clean hands. The Associated Press quoted Todd as declaring, "We think that working with technology experts, legal scholars, researchers and anti-spyware companies to establish such standards is a top priority." A priority, one would imagine, on par with avoiding the wrath of Spitzer. --Greg Levine

Background on Ballmer 		When to watch: Wednesday, June 15. How many takers will there be for downgraded software? Wednesday sees the launch of Microsoft's (nasdaq: MSFT - news - people ) Windows XP Home Edition N and Windows XP Professional N, both of which have been brought in to comply with a March 2004 European Union antitrust ruling. The new software is identical to the company's Windows XP package, except that it has no Windows Media Player software. Microsoft Chief Executive Steve Ballmer said on June 6 that the software giant took its responsibility to carry out the landmark antitrust ruling "very seriously," and that the company would "continue to focus on fulfilling all our obligations in every way we can." The software behemoth, headed by Chairman Bill Gates, has said the reduced media editions were originally released in January, but required modification after regulators intervened. The EU Commission has finally said that it was satisfied with plans to publish Windows excluding the media player. Windows Media Player software's main rivals are RealNetworks' (nasdaq: RNWK - news - people ) RealPlayer and Apple Computer's (nasdaq: AAPL - news - people ) QuickTime package. --Chris Noon
When to watch: Wednesday, June 15, and Thursday, June 16. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration next week will convene an advisory panel to take a close look at controversial issues of blood pressure and race. On Wednesday, the academics will discuss how doctors should weigh the risks and benefits of most kinds of blood pressure-lowering drugs, in a decision that could have import for big pharma like Pfizer (nyse: PFE - news - people ) and Novartis (nyse: NVS - news - people ). Billions of dollars in sales are at stake. But that's only the warm-up for Thursday's controversy, when NitroMed (nasdaq: NTMD - news - people ), a small biotech firm led by pharmaceutical veteran Michael D. Loberg, will present data on its drug BiDil. The heart-failure medicine is the first to show a survival benefit in African-American patients, but not in any other race. Expect hand-wringing from the panel, and nail-biting from Loberg and his colleagues. --Matthew Herper

Background on Chizen 		When to watch: Thursday, June 16. Press critic A.J. Liebling once observed, "Freedom of the press is guaranteed only to those who own one." Adobe Systems (nasdaq: ADBE - news - people ) changed that with its nifty desktop-publishing software--and did it profitably. On Thursday, Wall Street analysts expect Adobe Systems' Chief Executive Bruce R. Chizen to report second-quarter 2005 earnings of 27 cents per share, up from 22 cents per share in the same period a year ago. Last year's sales totaled about $1.6 billion, up nearly 29% from the previous year. Better yet, net income climbed to about $450.4 million in 2004, up almost 70% over 2003. Internet users know the company's Acrobat Reader, a free tool that displays PDF files. Adobe's publishing software includes Photoshop, Illustrator and PageMaker. The company also offers GoLive, a Web-design software package, and InDesign, which provides layout and design tools. Now everyone can be a publisher, as evidenced by the profusion of Web sites, Internet blogs and small special-interest magazines. But amid Adobe's whiz-bang software, don't forget Liebling, who is best remembered for his critiques of the press published in The New Yorker during the age of clanking presses and ink-stained wretches. Back in the days of hot type, Liebling cracked, "People everywhere confuse what they read in newspapers with news." --Scott Reeves
When to watch: Thursday, June 16. Goldman Sachs (nyse: GS - news - people ) Chief Financial Officer David A. Viniar will walk investors and analysts through second-quarter results Thursday, but already things aren't looking pretty. Analysts expect Goldman to turn in earnings per share of $1.96, well below the $2.31 per share it notched in the second quarter last year. A rough spring for fixed-income and equity trading and underwriting is partly to blame. Virtually every Wall Street bank has warned that profits won't be stellar. But for Goldman the quarter may leave a particularly bitter taste. Several clients pulled IPOs or had disappointing results during the quarter, including the much-anticipated initial public offering of boutique-investment bank Lazard (nyse: LAZ - news - people ). The stock was priced at $25 and failed to rise above it, falling on its very first day of trading, even as Goldman tried to support the price. Lazard trades at around $21 now. --Liz Moyer

Background on McCollough 		When to watch: Friday, June 17. Electronics retailers Best Buy (nyse: BBY - news - people ) and Circuit City Stores (nyse: CC - news - people ) both report quarterly earnings next week, and their fortunes couldn't be more different. Analysts expect Best Buy to post a profit of 30 cents per share on Tuesday--the company, which has been spending on a revamp and redesign of its stores, forecast a profit range of 27 to 32 cents per share. But keep your electronic eye on Circuit City--helmed by W. Alan McCollough--which hasn't provided earnings guidance for the quarter. According to consensus estimates, Circuit City is slated for a loss of about 2 cents per share when it reports on Friday. Despite its heavily promoted specials on flat-screen TVs, the company has been consistently losing market share to rival Best Buy. --Arik Hesseldahl
